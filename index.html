About Me

I am a highly motivated researcher in experimental condensed matter physics, specializing in materials physics and thin-film engineering. With extensive hands-on experience in Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) and material characterization, I am dedicated to exploring novel properties in quantum materials and their applications in spintronics and next-generation computing. My expertise encompasses the growth of diverse thin-film systems, including 2D layered materials, topological quantum materials, magnetic materials, and semiconductors. I am committed to addressing critical scientific challenges through innovative problem-solving and effective interdisciplinary collaboration.

Beyond my core research, I am an enthusiastic adopter of emerging technologies. I actively leverage AI tools to optimize research workflows, ranging from coding for efficient scientific data analysis to creating my website.

Currently, I am seeking R&D roles where I can apply my materials expertise to advance fundamental science or solve bottleneck challenges in next-generation computing and memory devices.

Projects

Van der Waals epitaxy of layered topological insulator Bi2Se3

This study reports the growth of high-quality topological insulator Bi2Se3 thin films on single-layer MoS2 via van der Waals epitaxy, achieving excellent crystallinity starting immediately from the first quintuple layer. In contrast to traditional 3D substrates, this method yields larger domain sizes and a 2–3 times enhancement in mobility, evidenced by the observation of Shubnikov–de Haas oscillations. Consequently, this approach of using a van der Waals type template presents a promising pathway for the growth of other low-dimensional layered materials.

MBE Electrical Transport Topological Insulator

Topological phase transition and Rashba-like preformed topological surface states of α-Sn thin films

This study investigates the electronic structure of compressively strained alpha-Sn(001) thin films, revealing a topological phase transition from a trivial to a nontrivial phase at a critical thickness of 6 bilayers. In films thicker than 30 bilayers, Rashba-like surface states were identified as preformed topological surface states that coexist with linearly dispersive topological surface states. Furthermore, measurements of thick 370-bilayer films confirm a Dirac semimetal phase, suggesting that the unique coexistence of these surface states offers a promising platform for spintronic applications.
